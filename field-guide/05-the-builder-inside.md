# Field Guide 05 — Five-primitive self-audit

Companion to Chapter 5, *The Builder Inside.*

The chapter argues that you already use the five leadership primitives every day on people: setting direction, judging quality, allocating attention, managing risk, building capacity. The shift is that you'll apply them to a multi-agent harness instead of a multi-human team. Same primitives. Different instrument.

Block twenty-five minutes.

## Part 1 — The map

For each primitive, write down two examples from the last month: one where you applied it to a person or team, and one where you could have applied it to an AI system but didn't.

- **Setting direction.** Where did you set the brief, scope, or success criteria for someone's work?
- **Judging quality.** Where did you read a draft, a deck, a result, and call whether it was good enough?
- **Allocating attention.** Where did you decide what to spend time on and what to defer?
- **Managing risk.** Where did you think through what could go wrong and protect against it?
- **Building capacity.** Where did you teach, coach, or upgrade someone's ability to do work without you?

Most readers find that for every primitive, there's at least one missed AI-side application from the last month.

## Part 2 — The re-description

Pick one decision you made in the last month. A real one, with stakes.

Now describe that decision twice:

- The first description in standard executive language. What you decided, why, who you brought along.
- The second description in builder-practice language. What spec did you set? What evidence did you weigh? What guardrail did you put in place? What downstream judgment did you delegate, and to whom or what?

The two descriptions should be the same decision. The second will surface things the first hides.

## Part 3 — The one transfer

Looking at Part 1 and Part 2, pick the single primitive where the transfer to AI-system management would compound fastest in your portfolio. One sentence on what that transfer looks like in practice.

Examples readers commonly land on:

- "I'll write the success criteria for our AI-assisted decisions before the work starts, the same way I do for humans."
- "I'll ask 'what could go wrong' on every AI-touched output, the same way I do for vendor work."
- "I'll spend Friday afternoons coaching the agents I've spawned, the same way I coach my team on Thursdays."

That sentence is your starting position for the next chapter, where the work begins.
