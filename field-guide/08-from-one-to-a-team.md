# Field Guide 08 — Three exercises for weeks six through ten

Companion to Chapter 8, *From One Operator to a Team.*

The chapter argues that what changes when a second operator joins you is not a scaling-up of the same practice. It's a new set of failure modes. Drift between operators' rigs. Conflict over which skills are shared. The premature-platform trap. This guide is three things you do during the four weeks that bring on a second operator.

## Week 1 — The second-operator onboard

Pick the second person.

Not a vendor. Not a contractor. Someone inside your domain who will operate the harness on real work, in your direct line of sight, for the next six months at least. The right second operator is the person who has been quietly trying to build with AI on their own and hasn't told you yet.

Sit with them for ninety minutes. Show them your rig. Walk through one of your skills end-to-end. Hand them a copy of slopless or your equivalent. Let them set up their own machine that afternoon while you're still in the room.

Success criterion: by Friday, the second operator has shipped one piece of real work through their harness.

## Weeks 2–3 — A three-failure-mode written agreement

Three failure modes recur in two-operator setups: drift (your skills and theirs evolve apart and stop being interchangeable), conflict (you both modify the same shared skill and disagree on direction), and premature platform (one of you proposes building infrastructure to manage the rigs before the rigs are stable).

Write a one-page agreement that names each failure mode and what you'll both do about it. Examples of the kind of thing the page should say:

- "We commit to running a one-hour rig review every other Friday for the next three months."
- "Shared skills live in one repo. Personal skills live in our own directories. We don't shared-edit each other's personal skills."
- "We don't propose a platform until at least two more operators have joined and the rig has stabilized for two months."

The agreement has to be written down. Verbal versions decay.

Success criterion: both operators have signed the page, and the next failure that fires has a named pre-agreed response.

## Week 4 — The platform-or-keep-going decision

At the end of week ten, sit down with the second operator and answer one question: do we build a platform now, or do we keep running personal rigs that share artifacts?

The chapter's argument is that nine times out of ten the answer is "keep going." Platforms built before the practice has stabilized institutionalize the wrong patterns. The kind of organization that will benefit from a platform has at least four operators, six months of stable rigs, and at least three repeated failure patterns that a platform could have prevented.

If your answer is "build a platform," document the three failure patterns it solves and the four-plus operators it serves. If those don't exist, the answer is keep going.

Success criterion: the decision is written down with its evidence. Whatever you decided, you'll know in three months whether you were right.

## After
