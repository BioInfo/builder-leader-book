# Field Guide 09 — Three checkpoints for month three and beyond

Companion to Chapter 9, *Leading From the Other Side.*

The chapter argues that builder-leader is a permanent role, not a transitional one. The crossing isn't a phase you exit. It's a posture you keep. This guide is three checkpoints you revisit quarterly to confirm the practice is still alive in you.

Block one hour. Bring honest answers.

## Checkpoint 1 — The three-shifts description

Ask three people who've worked closely with you for the last six months: name three things about how I lead that have shifted since I started building.

Don't prep them. Don't tell them what answer you're hoping for. Just ask.

Read what they say. The point isn't to validate yourself. It's to surface what others see that you don't. The most common shifts readers hear back:

- "You ask sharper questions about how the work will be done, not just what gets delivered."
- "You're slower to commission, faster to prototype."
- "You correct AI-related claims in meetings that you used to let pass."

If the three people can't name three shifts, the practice is hidden. That's the answer to a different question.

## Checkpoint 2 — The archetype audit

The chapter introduces a 2×2 archetype matrix: technical-fluency (low / high) crossed with portfolio-density (low / high). Four archetypes. Each has its own crossing path and its own permanent posture.

Place yourself on the matrix today. Then look back at where you placed three months ago, six months ago, twelve months ago.

The compounding move isn't from one archetype to another. It's deepening the same archetype with operating practice that reinforces it. Augmented-leadership writers will tell you to delegate the technical work to a specialist. The book's case is that for the executive whose surface area times daily-decision density warrants it, the operator practice is the work.

Write down where you are, where you want to be in twelve months, and the one operating habit that closes the gap. One sentence.

## Checkpoint 3 — The Fortune 500 CEO recognition question

The book describes a recognition pattern that operators report: a moment when someone you've crossed with — a peer, a direct report, a person you mentored — names what's changed in the practice without prompting. Not "you've been using AI a lot." A specific recognition of the operator stance itself.

Have you had that moment in the last quarter? Has anyone named the practice as a practice, in language that didn't exist for them before?

If yes: write down what they said and what triggered it. That moment is the strongest signal that the crossing has happened in your network, not just in you.

If not: the practice may be private. Bring it forward in the next quarter. Teach it. Show it. Let it be visible. The community of operators on the other side is real, and the book's last argument is that you're already part of it. Make sure the people around you know.

## After

The checkpoints are quarterly. Set a recurring calendar entry now. The book's job ends here. Yours doesn't.
